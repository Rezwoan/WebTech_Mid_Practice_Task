Life And Health In Bangladesh

Dhaka’s toxic air reshapes daily life

Published: November 24, 2025

With Dhaka ranked as the world's second most polluted city, residents are adjusting their daily routines around air‑quality alerts. Many people now check pollution levels before leaving home, children's outdoor activities are being cut back and health experts are seeing more patients with breathing problems on days when the smog is thickest.

Doctors advise wearing proper masks, avoiding outdoor exercise and keeping indoor air as clean as possible, but say long‑term solutions will require stricter controls on brick kilns, traffic emissions and construction dust.

Heatwaves hit health and incomes across Bangladesh

Published: August 29, 2025

New research suggests Bangladesh endured dozens of extra days of extreme heat between mid‑2024 and mid‑2025, a shift scientists attribute to human‑driven climate change. For low‑income residents, especially those living in crowded urban neighbourhoods, the hotter days translate into higher medical costs, lost work hours and rising electricity bills.

Community organisations are calling for more shaded public spaces, cooling centres and early‑warning systems so that people can better prepare for dangerous heat, while researchers warn that heat stress will only intensify without rapid cuts in global emissions.

Bangladesh marks World One Health Day with call for collaboration

Published: November 11, 2025

Bangladesh has joined countries around the world in celebrating World One Health Day 2025, highlighting the links between human, animal and environmental health. At events in Dhaka, officials and health experts stressed that preventing future pandemics and tackling antimicrobial resistance require doctors, veterinarians and environmental scientists to work more closely together.

The One Health approach is also being promoted in efforts to improve food safety and protect biodiversity, with national institutions pledging to strengthen data sharing and joint surveillance of emerging health threats.
